PROJECT 1

OCEANBOUND CLEAN UP

PROBLEMS

Ocean-bound trash refers to plastic and other waste materials that are at risk of entering the ocean from various sources on land. It includes litter, improperly disposed of waste, and debris that is transported through rivers, storm drains, or other pathways to coastal areas and eventually into the ocean. Ocean-bound trash, particularly plastic waste, poses a severe threat to marine ecosystems and wildlife. Once in the ocean, plastics can break down into smaller pieces called microplastics, which can be ingested by marine animals, leading to internal injuries, blockages, and even death. Additionally, larger debris can entangle marine creatures, causing physical harm and impairing their ability to swim, hunt, or reproduce.

SOLUTION

Addressing ocean-bound trash requires a multifaceted approach involving various stakeholders, governments, organizations, and individuals. Here are some key solutions to combat ocean-bound trash: Improved waste management: Implementing and improving waste management systems is essential. This includes establishing proper waste collection, sorting, and recycling infrastructure, as well as ensuring effective waste disposal facilities. Encouraging responsible waste management practices at both individual and industrial levels is crucial.

FISHING NET RECOVERY

PROBLEMS

Fishing gear - nets and ropes - pose a significant risk to sea life if cut away in the ocean. Unfortunately, incentives do not exist to recover this gear so at the end of its life, most gets landfilled or simply cut away at sea. It's a huge reason for why fishing nets are the largest ocean plastic polluter. Fishing net recovery, also known as ghost net recovery, is a critical aspect of addressing ocean pollution and protecting marine life. Ghost nets are abandoned, lost, or discarded fishing nets that continue to drift in the ocean, entangling and posing a threat to marine animals, coral reefs, and other underwater habitats.
